# Editorial Architecture — The Wealth Confidence Guide

This is the blueprint that drives the manuscript. It fixes the reader profile,
the page-by-page plan, per-chapter briefs, and the evidence / worksheet / image
maps before any drafting.

---

## 1. Final reader profile

**Public positioning:** age-inclusive. Never labeled by age.

**Internal primary reader:** a US adult, often ~45–70, who wants calmer, more
intentional everyday money decisions. May be employed, self-employed, working
irregular shifts, approaching or in retirement, managing a household, or
supporting family across generations. Deals with subscriptions, convenience and
online purchases, and spending during stress, tiredness, boredom, or habit.

**Assume the reader is:** capable, experienced, busy, and self-aware. **Never
assume** the reader is poor, in debt, irresponsible, retired, unintelligent, in
cognitive decline, or has failed with money.

**Voice:** calm, respectful, practical, plain American English. Grade 7–9.
Short paragraphs. One lesson at a time. Compassionate, never shaming.

**Emotional job of the book:** replace guilt and pressure with *awareness* and
*small, repeatable action* — building confidence (self-efficacy), not promising
money outcomes.

## 3. Chapter briefs (the seven days)

Each day answers the six required questions and contains all nine required
elements (lesson, why, example, action, Notice prompt, reflection question,
worksheet, citations, compassionate reminder).

### Day 1 — Notice One Spending Pattern
- **Objective:** See one repeated purchase without judging it.
- **Reader problem:** Many purchases happen on autopilot; "noticing" feels vague.
- **Lesson:** Much everyday behavior is repeated in the same context [@wood2002];
  habits are cue-triggered [@neal2006]. Simply *observing* is itself associated
  with progress [@harkin2016].
- **Example:** The mid-shift coffee-and-snack stop, same store, same time.
- **Action:** Pick one repeated purchase to watch for the day.
- **Notice prompt:** "What did I buy without really deciding today?"
- **Reflection:** "Where and when does this usually happen?"
- **Worksheet:** WS-1 spending-pattern observation.
- **Reminder:** Noticing is not judging. You are gathering information, not
  grading yourself.
- **Avoid overthinking:** Don't try to change anything yet.
- **Prohibited:** savings promises; "bad habit" language.

### Day 2 — Pause Before One Automatic Purchase
- **Objective:** Insert ten quiet seconds before one automatic buy.
- **Problem:** Purchases feel finished before we notice deciding.
- **Lesson:** A brief pause meets the "pain of paying" and individual differences
  in it [@rick2008]; situational factors (time, feelings) precede impulse buys
  [@beatty1998].
- **Example:** Hovering over "Buy now" while tired at night.
- **Action:** Choose one recurring moment; add a 10-second pause.
- **Notice prompt:** "What am I feeling right now — and do I still want this in a
  minute?"
- **Reflection:** "What happened when I paused?"
- **Worksheet:** WS-2 purchase pause.
- **Reminder:** A pause is not a "no." Sometimes you'll still buy — that's fine.
- **Prohibited:** implying pausing saves a set amount.

### Day 3 — Understand the Moment Around the Purchase
- **Objective:** Map the *situation* around a purchase (trigger map).
- **Problem:** We blame willpower; the situation is often the driver.
- **Lesson:** Cues drive habits [@neal2006]; browsing/time/feelings precede impulse
  buys [@beatty1998]; money stress can occupy attention [@shah2012] (general, not
  personal).
- **Example:** Boredom + phone + free evening → scrolling a shopping app.
- **Action:** Fill a simple trigger map for one purchase.
- **Notice prompt:** "What was happening around me just before?"
- **Reflection:** "Which part of the situation would be easiest to change?"
- **Worksheet:** WS-3 trigger map.
- **Reminder:** Triggers are information, not faults.
- **Prohibited:** medical/anxiety claims; labeling the reader as stressed/poor.

### Day 4 — Choose One Small Shift
- **Objective:** Turn one observation into a specific when-then plan.
- **Problem:** Big resolutions collapse; vague intentions fade.
- **Lesson:** Specific if-then plans improve follow-through [@gollwitzer1999;
  @gollwitzer2006].
- **Example:** "When I open the shopping app after dinner, then I'll add items to
  a list and check tomorrow."
- **Action:** Write one when-then shift.
- **Notice prompt:** "What is one small, realistic change I could try?"
- **Reflection:** "Is my plan specific enough to picture?"
- **Worksheet:** WS-4 one-small-shift plan.
- **Reminder:** Small and doable beats big and abandoned.
- **Prohibited:** "guaranteed"; outcome promises.

### Day 5 — Make the Easier Choice More Visible
- **Objective:** Adjust the environment so the calmer choice is the easy one.
- **Problem:** Willpower is unreliable; friction/defaults quietly steer us.
- **Lesson:** Defaults strongly shape choices [@johnson2003]; cues trigger habits
  [@neal2006] — so change the cues and the ease.
- **Example:** Logging out of stored payment; moving the app off the home screen.
- **Action:** Add friction to one automatic purchase; ease one calmer choice.
- **Notice prompt:** "What makes this purchase so easy right now?"
- **Reflection:** "What one change made the calmer choice easier?"
- **Worksheet:** WS-5 environment & friction checklist.
- **Reminder:** You're designing your surroundings, not testing your willpower.
- **Prohibited:** none specific beyond global rules.

### Day 6 — Repeat Without Expecting Perfection
- **Objective:** Keep the practice going through normal misses.
- **Problem:** One missed day feels like failure and ends the effort.
- **Lesson:** Habits form over time and vary widely; missing one day did not stop
  formation [@lally2010]. Self-kindness after a slip supports motivation
  [@breines2012].
- **Example:** A skipped day during a busy week — resumed the next morning.
- **Action:** Track repetition lightly; plan for a likely miss.
- **Notice prompt:** "Did I practice today — yes or not yet?"
- **Reflection:** "What helped me come back after a miss?"
- **Worksheet:** WS-6 repetition tracker.
- **Reminder:** Missing a day is part of the process, not the end of it.
- **Prohibited:** "21 days" myth; "never miss" language.

### Day 7 — Build Your Personal Awareness Plan
- **Objective:** Combine the week into a personal, repeatable plan.
- **Problem:** Insights fade without a simple structure.
- **Lesson:** When-then plans aid follow-through [@gollwitzer2006]; confidence in
  one's ability (self-efficacy) supports effort and persistence [@bandura1977].
- **Example:** One pattern watched, one pause, one shift, one environment change.
- **Action:** Complete a one-page awareness plan.
- **Notice prompt:** "Which one habit is worth keeping?"
- **Reflection:** "What will I do on a hard day?"
- **Worksheet:** WS-7 personal awareness plan.
- **Reminder:** This is your plan; adjust it freely.
- **Prohibited:** financial-freedom/retirement promises.
