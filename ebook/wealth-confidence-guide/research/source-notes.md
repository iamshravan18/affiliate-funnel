# Source Notes — The Wealth Confidence Guide

Discovery: OpenAlex (multiple focused queries). Metadata verified independently
via Crossref (by DOI) or on official government domains. Discovery ≠ proof: each
source below was read at the abstract/record level and selected only after
confirming it supports the *specific* idea cited. Hedged wording is used
throughout the manuscript (RULE 5). "What it does NOT support" is recorded to
prevent overreach.

**Totals:** 18 sources — 16 peer-reviewed, 2 US government.

---

## Habit & automaticity

### Wood, Quinn & Kashy (2002) — *Habits in everyday life: Thought, emotion, and action.* JPSP 83(6), 1281–1297. DOI 10.1037/0022-3514.83.6.1281
- **Reason selected:** Direct evidence that a large portion of everyday behavior
  is repeated in the same context (diary studies).
- **Idea supported:** Much daily behavior is habitual — performed often and in
  the same location, with different thought/emotion patterns than deliberate acts.
- **Does NOT support:** Any specific figure about *spending*; causation; that
  habits are "bad."
- **Limitation:** Diary self-report; community/student samples; correlational.
- **Placement:** Day 1; Notice framework.

### Neal, Wood & Quinn (2006) — *Habits—A Repeat Performance.* Current Directions in Psychological Science 15(4), 198–202. DOI 10.1111/j.1467-8721.2006.00435.x
- **Reason selected:** Clear, citable statement that habits are cue-triggered and
  automatic.
- **Idea supported:** Habits are activated automatically by context cues that
  co-occurred with past responses.
- **Does NOT support:** That cues *force* behavior, or that awareness alone
  changes it.
- **Limitation:** Review/theory; not spending-specific.
- **Placement:** Day 1; Day 3; Day 5.

### Lally, van Jaarsveld, Potts & Wardle (2010) — *How are habits formed: Modelling habit formation in the real world.* European Journal of Social Psychology 40(6), 998–1009. DOI 10.1002/ejsp.674
- **Reason selected:** Real-world habit-formation timing; corrects the popular
  "21 days" myth and supports the "missing one day is okay" message.
- **Idea supported:** Median time to automaticity ≈ 66 days (range 18–254);
  missing a single day did not materially harm habit formation.
- **Does NOT support:** A fixed "N days to a habit"; that all habits form the
  same way; anything about money habits specifically.
- **Limitation:** 82 participants analyzed; self-report habit index; simple
  eating/drinking/activity behaviors.
- **Placement:** Day 6; Your next 30 days; Compassionate restart.

## Self-monitoring & planning

### Harkin, Webb, Chang, Prestwich, Conner, Kellar, Benn & Sheeran (2016) — *Does monitoring goal progress promote goal attainment? A meta-analysis of the experimental evidence.* Psychological Bulletin 142(2), 198–229. DOI 10.1037/bul0000025
- **Reason selected:** Meta-analytic support for the core "notice / self-monitor"
  premise.
- **Idea supported:** Prompting people to monitor goal progress had a significant
  effect on goal attainment.
- **Does NOT support:** That monitoring *spending* guarantees savings; effects are
  averages and vary.
- **Limitation:** Mostly health/eating/behavioral goals; applying to money is by
  analogy (noted in text).
- **Placement:** Day 1; Seven-day reflection; Day 6.

### Gollwitzer (1999) — *Implementation intentions: Strong effects of simple plans.* American Psychologist 54(7), 493–503. DOI 10.1037/0003-066X.54.7.493
### Gollwitzer & Sheeran (2006) — *Implementation Intentions and Goal Achievement: A Meta-analysis of Effects and Processes.* Advances in Experimental Social Psychology 38, 69–119. DOI 10.1016/S0065-2601(06)38002-1
- **Reason selected:** Foundational statement + meta-analysis for "if-then"
  planning, which underpins Days 4 and 7.
- **Idea supported:** Specific when-then plans improve the chance of acting on an
  intention; meta-analysis found a medium-to-large average effect on goal
  attainment.
- **Does NOT support:** That plans work equally in every situation, or produce a
  financial result.
- **Limitation:** Effect sizes vary by domain; averages across many goal types.
- **Placement:** Day 4; Day 7.

## Choice architecture, impulse & online buying

### Johnson & Goldstein (2003) — *Do Defaults Save Lives?* Science 302(5649), 1338–1339. DOI 10.1126/science.1091721
- **Reason selected:** Widely-cited demonstration of the power of defaults, which
  motivates the Day 5 "make the easier choice more visible" idea.
- **Idea supported:** Default options can lead to striking differences in outcomes.
- **Does NOT support:** Anything about spending defaults specifically (organ-donation
  policy context) — used as an illustrative principle.
- **Limitation:** Policy/organ-donation domain.
- **Placement:** Day 5.

### Beatty & Ferrell (1998) — *Impulse buying: Modeling its precursors.* Journal of Retailing 74(2), 169–191. DOI 10.1016/S0022-4359(99)80092-X
- **Reason selected:** Names concrete situational precursors of impulse buying.
- **Idea supported:** Time available, browsing, and in-the-moment positive feelings
  precede impulse purchases.
- **Does NOT support:** That these *cause* every unplanned purchase; modern online
  context.
- **Limitation:** Single study; 1990s mall shoppers; correlational model.
- **Placement:** Day 2; Day 3.

### Verhagen & van Dolen (2011) — *The influence of online store beliefs on consumer online impulse buying.* Information & Management 48(8), 320–327. DOI 10.1016/j.im.2011.08.001
- **Reason selected:** Extends the impulse idea to online environments.
- **Idea supported:** Online store characteristics/beliefs relate to online impulse
  buying.
- **Does NOT support:** Causation; generalization beyond the studied retailer.
- **Limitation:** Single study; one retailer; correlational (SEM).
- **Placement:** Online shopping & convenience spending.

### Rick, Cryder & Loewenstein (2008) — *Tightwads and Spendthrifts.* Journal of Consumer Research 34(6), 767–782. DOI 10.1086/523285
- **Reason selected:** Grounds the "pause" idea in the felt experience of spending
  and normalizes individual differences (no shame).
- **Idea supported:** An anticipatory "pain of paying" is linked to spending less;
  people differ widely (tightwads vs. spendthrifts).
- **Does NOT support:** That one type is "better"; any prescription to feel more/less
  pain.
- **Limitation:** Trait measure; correlational; US samples.
- **Placement:** Day 2; Common moments.

## Financial stress & attention

### Shah, Mullainathan & Shafir (2012) — *Some Consequences of Having Too Little.* Science 338(6107), 682–685. DOI 10.1126/science.1222426
### Mani, Mullainathan, Shafir & Zhao (2013) — *Poverty Impedes Cognitive Function.* Science 341(6149), 976–980. DOI 10.1126/science.1238041
- **Reason selected:** Supports the gentle framing that money worry can occupy
  attention — used generally, never to label the reader.
- **Idea supported:** Scarcity/financial worry can change attention and engagement
  and can occupy cognitive capacity.
- **Does NOT support:** That the reader is poor or impaired; a permanent effect;
  that awareness "fixes" it. **A published re-analysis (Wicherts & Zand Scholten,
  2013) questioned parts of Mani et al.** — recorded as a limitation; wording kept
  cautious and general.
- **Limitation:** Studied in scarcity/low-income contexts; some findings contested;
  lab-induced + correlational.
- **Placement:** Day 3; Welcome (general framing only).

## Self-compassion & restarting

### Breines & Chen (2012) — *Self-Compassion Increases Self-Improvement Motivation.* PSPB 38(9), 1133–1143. DOI 10.1177/0146167212445599
- **Reason selected:** Evidence base for the compassionate-restart message.
- **Idea supported:** Self-compassion after a mistake increased motivation to
  improve (four experiments).
- **Does NOT support:** That self-compassion changes financial outcomes; long-term
  effects.
- **Limitation:** Lab experiments; mostly students; not money-specific.
- **Placement:** Compassionate restart; Day 6.

### Sirois (2014) — *Procrastination and Stress: Exploring the Role of Self-compassion.* Self and Identity 13(2), 128–145. DOI 10.1080/15298868.2013.763404
- **Reason selected:** Links self-compassion to lower stress around self-regulation
  slips.
- **Idea supported:** Higher self-compassion was associated with lower stress in the
  procrastination–stress relationship.
- **Does NOT support:** Causation; a spending application.
- **Limitation:** Correlational; procrastination context.
- **Placement:** Compassionate restart.

## Confidence & well-being framing

### Bandura (1977) — *Self-efficacy: Toward a unifying theory of behavioral change.* Psychological Review 84(2), 191–215. DOI 10.1037/0033-295X.84.2.191
- **Reason selected:** Explains why the guide centers *confidence* ("wealth
  confidence") rather than outcomes.
- **Idea supported:** Perceived self-efficacy influences effort and persistence.
- **Does NOT support:** A money-specific claim; that confidence alone changes
  finances.
- **Limitation:** Foundational, broad theory.
- **Placement:** Welcome; Day 7.

### Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (2015) — *Financial well-being: The goal of financial education.* consumerfinance.gov
- **Reason selected:** Authoritative, government definition of "financial
  well-being" that frames the guide's purpose without promising outcomes.
- **Idea supported:** Financial well-being = meeting obligations, feeling secure,
  and having freedom of choice; it is personal and subjective.
- **Does NOT support:** That this guide (or any activity) raises well-being.
- **Limitation:** Framework/definition, not an intervention study.
- **Placement:** Welcome; Day 7.

### Federal Trade Commission — *Getting In and Out of Free Trials, Auto-Renewals, and Negative Option Subscriptions.* consumer.ftc.gov (Consumer Advice)
- **Reason selected:** Neutral, authoritative consumer guidance on auto-renewals.
- **Idea supported:** Subscriptions often auto-renew unless canceled; know the terms,
  watch for pre-checked boxes, and note how to cancel.
- **Does NOT support:** Any claim that canceling subscriptions produces a specific
  saving.
- **Limitation:** Consumer-education guidance, not a study.
- **Placement:** Recurring purchases & automatic renewals.
